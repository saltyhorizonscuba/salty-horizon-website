Where to Dive in Costa Rica: Comparing Its Very Different Underwater Worlds

"Diving in Costa Rica" can mean a 36-hour ocean crossing to a shark-filled seamount, or a private half-day boat trip from a beach town. Here's how the country's main dive regions actually compare.

Salty HorizonJuly 14, 20268 min read

Ask three divers what "diving in Costa Rica" is like and you may get three unrelated answers, because the country doesn't have one dive scene — it has several, separated by hundreds of kilometres of ocean and, in some cases, an entire order of magnitude in price. Before booking a trip, it helps to know which one you're actually signing up for.

Isla del Coco: the bucket-list crossing

Cocos Island sits roughly 550 km (340 miles) off Costa Rica's Pacific coast — closer to the Galápagos than to the mainland it belongs to. There is no airstrip and no hotel; overnight stays on the island itself are prohibited, so the only way to dive it is a liveaboard vessel, and the crossing from Puntarenas alone takes 30 to 36 hours each way.2 UNESCO inscribed Cocos Island National Park as a World Heritage Site in 1997, citing the critical habitat it provides for large pelagic species, and expanded the protected marine zone to nearly 2,000 km² in 2002.1

What that isolation buys divers is scale: schooling scalloped hammerheads reported in the hundreds, along with Galápagos sharks, silky sharks, manta rays and whale sharks passing through nutrient-rich upwellings around its underwater seamounts.2 It isn't a casual add-on to a beach holiday — expeditions run 8 to 11 days, cost roughly $5,000 to $9,500 per person, and most operators require Advanced Open Water certification with 50 or more logged dives before they'll take you.2

Caño Island: the accessible alternative

Off the Osa Peninsula in the country's south, Caño Island is what most dive guides call the next best thing to Cocos — without the ocean crossing. It sits about 16 km (10 miles) offshore, reachable by day boat from Uvita (roughly 60–75 minutes) or the closer Drake Bay (about 30 minutes), with two-tank trips typically priced between $170 and $250.3 Divers there commonly see manta rays, dolphins, turtles and whitetip reef sharks, plus humpback whales and pilot whales in season — a strong day trip for intermediate divers rather than a specialist expedition.3

"Advanced divers chasing bucket-list experiences should prioritize Cocos Island and the Bat Islands; intermediate divers will love Caño Island's balance of world-class marine life and manageable conditions." Costa Rica Divers, dive-site comparison guide

The Catalinas and Bat Islands: Guanacaste's private day boats

Up north in Guanacaste, off Tamarindo, the Catalina Islands and Bat Islands (Islas Murciélago) work on an entirely different model again: no liveaboard, no multi-day expedition — just a private boat ride of around 40 minutes from shore. The Catalinas are known for giant Pacific manta rays, most reliably sighted from December to May, alongside eagle rays, reef sharks and large schools of fish. The Bat Islands, roughly 30 minutes further and within the Marine Sector of SINAC's Área de Conservación Guanacaste, are one of the few places anywhere with regular bull shark encounters, accessible from May to November and reserved for certified Advanced Open Water divers given the depth and current there.

Unlike Cocos or Caño, this region isn't built around a single dedicated dive operator's boat schedule — outfits like Salty Horizon run it as a private charter, so the day is built around one group rather than a fixed departure with strangers. It's the option that asks the least of your calendar and the most modest budget of the three, without giving up the two headline draws — mantas and bull sharks — that usually mean a trip much further offshore.

Matching the destination to the diver

In practice, the choice mostly comes down to three questions: how much time do you have, what's your certification level, and which specific animal is non-negotiable? A week-long liveaboard budget and an Advanced certification with 50+ dives point toward Cocos. A day or two on the Osa Peninsula, with intermediate skills, points toward Caño. And a shorter Guanacaste trip — private, no liveaboard required, mantas in season and bull sharks for the certified — covers the two encounters most visitors actually travel to Costa Rica hoping to have.
